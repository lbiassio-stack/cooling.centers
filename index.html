Cooling Center Accessibility in Seattle

Extreme heat events are becoming more frequent and intense as climate change progresses, creating increasing public health risks in cities. Cooling centers—publicly accessible, air-conditioned buildings—play a critical role in protecting residents during extreme heat. However, there is currently no centralized, up-to-date spatial resource that helps Seattle residents identify buildings they can access during extreme temperature events.

This project investigates the availability of buildings that could function as cooling centers during extreme weather events in Seattle. Potential cooling facilities include libraries, community centers, public schools, and other public buildings where residents may be able to seek relief during heat waves. By mapping these locations across the city, the project aims to better understand the spatial distribution of cooling infrastructure.

The website includes an interactive map that allows users to explore cooling center locations and evaluate their accessibility. Users can enter an address or select a location on the map to estimate the distance to the nearest cooling facility. In this pilot project, accessibility is operationalized using Euclidean distance between locations, recognizing that transportation systems may be disrupted during extreme weather conditions.

In addition to facility locations, the project compares the distribution of cooling centers with demographic and socioeconomic characteristics across Seattle. Overlay maps include population density, income levels, and racial demographics in order to identify potential inequities in access to cooling resources.

The spatial database used for this project integrates multiple GIS datasets. Facility data includes Seattle Parks and Recreation community centers, pools, spray parks, wading pools, lifeguard beaches, public libraries, senior centers, homeless shelters, and major public facilities. Most datasets are sourced from the City of Seattle’s open GIS portals, while additional facilities are manually identified where official datasets are unavailable.

Demographic characteristics are incorporated using American Community Survey data at the census tract level, including population, race, and income indicators. Transit accessibility is represented using route and stop data from regional transit datasets provided by King County, allowing analysis of proximity to frequent transit near potential cooling centers.

The results of this project will produce a series of “Cooling Vulnerability Maps” that highlight neighborhoods where socially vulnerable populations overlap with limited access to cooling facilities. By identifying these potential “cooling deserts,” the project can help inform climate resilience planning in Seattle and support decisions about where to deploy mobile cooling units or expand access to existing facilities.
